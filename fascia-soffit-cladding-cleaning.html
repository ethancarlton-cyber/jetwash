Why a tidy house can still look neglected

The short answer: over a year or two the white uPVC boards along your roofline collect green algae, lichen, traffic film and those tell-tale black "tide-line" streaks where rainwater runs off the roof and drips off the gutter lip. Even with a clean drive and tidy garden, a streaky grey-green roofline makes the whole house look tired. A fascia, soffit and cladding clean restores it to near-white, without the four-figure cost of replacing the boards. Because we are already at roofline, it is almost always done in the same visit as your gutters, with one access setup.

What we clean for the price

A proper roofline clean is more than a quick wipe of the front boards. It is the fascia (the vertical board your gutter is bolted to), the soffit underneath, the gutter exterior, and any uPVC or cladding on the walls, all treated with the method that suits each surface.

  • uPVC, aluminium and timber fascias & soffits soft-washed back to near-white
  • Black drip-streak ("tide line") and green algae removal with a dilute biocide / soft-wash solution
  • Gutter exterior washed in the same pass (we are already at roofline)
  • Soffit ventilation slots cleared so the roof space can still breathe
  • uPVC, composite and timber-look cladding & weatherboarding soft-washed (never high-pressure)
  • Pure-water rinse and microfibre wipe-down for a spot-free, streak-free finish
  • Optional anti-green biocide treatment to slow regrowth between visits

Why we never high-pressure uPVC or cladding

uPVC, composite and aluminium boards must not be high-pressure washed. Excess pressure cracks panels, lifts paint, breaches the seals and can force water behind the boards and into the roof void. It is one of the most common ways a DIY job ends up costing more than it saved. We use a controlled soft-wash and water-fed-pole method instead: a dilute biocide does the work of killing and lifting the algae, and purified water rinses it clean. We also avoid abrasive pads and neat bleach, which scratch and dull uPVC and degrade the rubber seals over time.

Why it must be done right

Get the method wrong on a roofline and you do not just fail to clean it, you damage it. uPVC fascias and soffits are thin plastic, often sun-brittled after years in the weather, and the manufacturers' own care guidance is the same across the trade: clean with mild soapy water or a dedicated uPVC cleaner and a soft cloth, never abrasive pads, scouring cream or solvents, which scratch the surface and degrade the rubber gaskets. That is why the gutter exterior, fascias and soffits get a controlled soft-wash and a pure-water rinse, not a pressure lance. There is a structural reason too: the soffit usually carries ventilation slots feeding air into the loft, and when algae and grime block them, that airflow is restricted and moisture gets trapped against the roof timbers. Block those vents and a cosmetic job quietly turns into a damp and rot problem. Done properly from the ground, the boards come back to near-white and the loft keeps breathing.

Common mistakes with fascia-soffit cleaning

Most of the damaged rooflines we are called out to fix come down to the same handful of errors:

  • Pressure-blasting the uPVC. High pressure cracks panels, lifts paint and breaches the joints between boards, and worst of all it forces water up behind the fascia into the roof void where it sits against the timbers. A clean turns into a leak.
  • Neat bleach or abrasive pads. Wire wool, scouring cream and undiluted bleach permanently dull the surface and rot the rubber seals, so the boards end up looking worse and leaking at the joints. Plastic does not buff back.
  • Leaning a ladder on the plastic gutter. A ladder against a uPVC gutter cracks it or pulls the bracket off the fascia, and it is a real fall risk. Reach-and-wash from the ground avoids both.
  • Skipping the gutters. Clean the fascia but leave the gutter overflowing and the streaks are back within months, because the run-off dripping off the gutter lip is what causes them in the first place.

Avoid those four and the job lasts. Get one wrong and you pay twice.

Why fascias go black and green

The short answer: the streaking is living algae and rainwater run-off tide-lines off the gutter lip, not removable grime, worst on north-facing, shaded and tree-lined elevations, where Surrey's leafy roads and traffic film grey the boards within one to two years. It responds to a biocide soft-wash, not scrubbing.

The black streaks are not dirt you can scrub off, they are algae and rainwater run-off "tide lines" dripping off the lip of the gutter, worst on north-facing, shaded and tree-lined elevations. Surrey's leafy roads (oak, sycamore, lime) and the traffic film off busy roads and the Gatwick corridor grey the boards faster than you would expect, often within one to two years. They respond to a biocide soft-wash rather than scrubbing, which is why the right chemistry matters more than brute force. There is a maintenance angle too: algae-clogged soffit ventilation slots restrict roof-space airflow and trap moisture, which over time can lead to damp and warped roof timbers, so a clean is partly looking after the roof, not just the look of it.

Done from the ground, no ladders, no scaffold cost

For most two- and three-storey homes we clean entirely from the ground with a water-fed pole. That means no ladders leaning against your guttering (a common cause of gutter and fascia damage in the first place), no scaffold hire, and a safer, tidier job. Working along a roofline carries a real fall risk, so reach-and-wash removes most of that. Genuinely awkward or very high rooflines, conservatories or tricky angles may need a scaffold tower or cherry picker, if yours does, we will tell you up front and price it in (typically an extra £100–£200), never spring it on you on the day.

Cleaning vs replacing

Replacement fascias and soffits run well into four figures once you add labour and access. A clean restores most boards to near-white for a fraction of that, which is why a clean is almost always the value play unless the boards are physically failing. If yours genuinely need replacing rather than cleaning, we will say so, we would rather point you to a roofer than take money for a job that won't last.

A kerb-appeal win before selling or letting

If you are putting a house on the market or preparing it for a tenant, the roofline is one of the first things a buyer's eye lands on. Pairing a fascia, soffit and cladding clean with a driveway clean or render clean gives the whole exterior a refresh for far less than most people expect, a strong, cheap win on the photos and the first impression.

How often should it be done?

Once a year is ideal for most homes, or twice a year if you are near trees or get heavy rain, roughly the same schedule as your gutters, which is another reason the two are done together. An optional anti-green biocide treatment after cleaning slows the algae coming back and stretches the interval.

Fascia, soffit & cladding cleaning FAQs

Most-asked questions from Surrey customers.

How much does gutter, fascia and soffit cleaning cost?

For most Surrey homes expect around £90–150 for a typical 2–3 bed semi and £150–250 for a larger detached, usually quoted together with your gutters as one bundle. Small bungalows start around £50–90, large or multi-storey runs £250–350+. We give a fixed price up front, call 01737 652 515.

Can you get rid of the black streaks and green algae?

Yes. The black drip-marks and green algae are the most common complaint, and our soft-wash solution and pure-water system lift them right off, leaving the boards back to near-white rather than just scrubbing the surface.

Do you use a pressure washer on uPVC fascias?

No. uPVC and cladding should never be high-pressure washed, it can crack panels, breach seals and force water behind them into the roof void. We use a soft-wash / water-fed-pole method designed for these surfaces.

Do you clean the gutters at the same time?

Almost always, yes. We’re already at roofline with the same kit, so we clear the gutters, wash the gutter exterior and clean the fascias and soffits in one visit, and it works out cheaper as a package.

Should fascias be cleaned with the gutters?

Yes, and it’s the cheaper way round. The expensive part of a roofline job is setting up access at height, not the cleaning, so doing the gutter clear, gutter exterior, fascias and soffits in one go means one call-out instead of two. A cleared gutter above a streaky black fascia also just looks half-finished.

Will you need ladders or scaffolding on my house?

For most 2–3 storey homes we clean entirely from the ground with a water-fed pole, so no ladders against your gutters and no scaffold cost. Awkward or very high rooflines may need extra access, which we’ll flag before we start.

How often should fascias and soffits be cleaned?

Once a year is ideal for most homes, or twice a year if you’re near trees or get heavy rain, roughly the same schedule as your gutters.

Do you clean cladding and weatherboarding too?

Yes. uPVC, composite and timber-look cladding are soft-washed with a low-pressure biocide treatment, and we can add an optional anti-green treatment to keep it cleaner for longer.

What is cladding cleaning cost per m2?

Most cladding soft-washing is priced by elevation rather than strictly per m2, typically from around £300 per elevation for a facade. Short partial runs are sometimes quoted by the linear metre at roughly £3–6/m, with one Surrey firm listing gutter, fascia, soffit and cladding at £5/m. As a rough per-m2 equivalent that works out around £8 to £15/m2, though we usually price cladding per elevation. For a whole wall a fixed price is usually simpler and cheaper.

How do you clean uPVC fascias?

With a soft-wash, not a pressure washer. A dilute biocide is applied low-pressure to kill and lift the algae, given a little dwell time, then a water-fed pole rinses with purified water so the boards dry spot-free and near-white. For low boards within ground reach, mild soapy water or a dedicated uPVC cleaner and a soft cloth does the job, never abrasive pads or neat bleach.

How do you clean white plastic soffits?

The same gentle way as the fascias: a low-pressure soft-wash and pure-water rinse, never high pressure, which forces water up behind the boards. Keep the soffit ventilation slots clear so the loft can breathe. Green algae on shaded, north-facing soffits lifts off with a biocide rather than scrubbing, which only smears it around.
