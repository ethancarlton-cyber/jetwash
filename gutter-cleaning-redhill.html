Gutter cleaning is the removal of leaves, moss and silt from roof gutters and the flushing of each downpipe so rainwater drains freely to the ground. A downpipe flush means running water through the vertical pipe and confirming it reaches the drain, the step that stops a gutter overflowing again weeks after the runs are cleared.

Why Redhill gutters block faster than the chalk up the hill

Redhill sits on the floor of the Vale of Holmesdale, the low, damp corridor between the North Downs Chalk and the Lower Greensand ridge, a boundary the British Geological Survey maps clearly through the town[2]. That valley humidity is brilliant for one thing: keeping algae, moss and leaf-mulch quietly composting in your gutters all year round. Drive eight miles north to Banstead on the chalk and gutters stay drier; down here on the valley floor they hold water and silt for longer, and the muck sets in the runs like wet porridge.

Add the trees. The older terraces off the high street and around Redstone Hill, the streets backing onto Memorial Park, and a good chunk of Earlswood Common back onto mature oak, sycamore and lime. Lovely in June. In October they drop a fortnight of leaf-fall straight into your gutter, and that’s before the sycamore seeds and the lime sticky stuff. We clear more leaf-packed RH1 gutters between October and December than the rest of the year combined.

What we actually see on Redhill gutters

  • Cast-iron gutters on the Victorian terraces. Plenty of the original housing stock around Redstone Hill and the streets off the station still has period cast-iron or steel gutter. Heavier, rust-prone at the joints, and the brackets work loose. We hand-clear those carefully and flag any joint that’s past a reseal.
  • Tight uPVC downpipes on the post-war semis. Merstham, Salfords and the inter-war Redhill semis tend to have narrower downpipes that pack solid with silt. Clear the gutter, miss the downpipe, and you’re overflowing again within a fortnight. We flush every one.
  • Algae streaks down the render. A lot of newer RH1 stock is painted or silicone render, and the valley humidity throws constant green streaks down the wall under any leaking gutter joint. Tells us exactly which run is overflowing before we’re even up the ladder.
  • Three-storey terraces on the slopes. Redhill isn’t flat, the streets climbing Redstone Hill mean awkward, steep ladder angles. That’s precisely where we reach for the ground-based vacuum instead.

The Earlswood & Salfords overflow problem nobody mentions

Parts of lower RH1, around Earlswood Common, the Salfords corridor and Petridge Wood, sit in documented surface-water flood pockets on the Environment Agency’s long-term flood-risk map[1]. When the ground’s already saturated in a wet Surrey winter, a blocked gutter overflowing down the wall is the last thing those properties need, the water has nowhere to go and it goes straight into the brickwork. If you’re in one of those pockets, a once-a-year clean before the autumn rains is genuinely the cheapest insurance on the house.

How a Redhill gutter clean works

People ask why we lean on a ground-based vacuum so much on the steep Redstone Hill streets. The honest answer is the law and common sense both point the same way. Under the Work at Height Regulations 2005, the duty is to avoid working at height where it's reasonably practicable, and to use equipment that lets you do the job from the ground when you can[3]. Our gutter vacuum reaches roof gutters up to around 12m, which covers every two and three-storey terrace on the slopes here, so no ladder need lean against your wall or a fragile fascia. We still go up the ladder for a compacted moss mat or a bird's nest the vacuum can't shift, but for the routine clear, ground level is safer, faster and cheaper for everyone. Most RH1 jobs come in inside two hours.

  1. Walk the perimeter. Check every run, spot the blockages, note loose brackets and any joint that’s gone, and on RH1 terraces, check whether it’s cast-iron or uPVC before we touch it.
  2. Clear, vacuum or hands-on. Ground-based gutter vacuum reaches up to around 12m, so there’s no ladder leaning on your wall, safer on the steep Redstone Hill streets and kinder to period terraces. Compacted moss mats and the inevitable bird’s nest get cleared by hand off the ladder. Most jobs end up a hybrid.
  3. Flush every downpipe. Hose each one and confirm the water reaches the drain. This is the bit most cleans skip, and it’s the usual reason a Redhill gutter overflows again weeks later.
  4. Quick fixes & honest report. Reseat a loose bracket, reseal a leaking joint while we’re up there. If it’s bigger, a rotten fascia or a run that needs replacing, we tell you straight and point you to a roofer rather than bodge it.

We bag the leaves, moss and silt and take it away, no scattering it across your borders. Most RH1 homes are done inside two hours, downpipe flush and minor fixes included.
