Thames Water hosepipe ban 2026: what it covers

Thames Water’s Temporary Use Ban has been in force since 00:01 on 23 July 2026 across its entire supply area, roughly 15 million customers spanning London, the Thames Valley and the Home Counties, including west and north Surrey towns such as Guildford, Walton-on-Thames, Esher and Kingston[8]. It bans domestic hosepipe use for watering gardens, washing cars, filling pools, and cleaning patios, paths and walls, but professional cleaning continues under the business-use exemption written into the notice itself. If you’re on SES Water, in Redhill, Reigate or east Surrey, none of this applies to you.

One line worth sitting with: the reason our area is fine isn’t luck, it’s geology. SES Water pulls roughly 85% of its supply from underground aquifers in the chalk and greensand, with only 15% from its reservoir at Bough Beech[2]. Aquifers refill slowly and drain slowly, so one scorching June doesn’t empty them the way it empties a reservoir. That’s why Guildford and Woking are under a ban and Redhill isn’t, a few miles apart.

What a hosepipe ban actually restricts

Worth knowing what a Temporary Use Ban actually restricts, so the headlines don’t catch you out. Under section 76 of the Water Industry Act 1991, a ban stops domestic hosepipe use: watering the garden or plants with a hosepipe, washing the car or boat, filling pools, ponds and fountains, cleaning walls or windows of your house, and cleaning paths, patios and other artificial outdoor surfaces[4]. Breaking it is a criminal offence with a fine of up to £1,000, with no end date on any of the current bans, they run until further notice and are reviewed against water levels.

Two things people miss. First, it’s the hosepipe that’s banned, not the water: a bucket and a watering can stay legal, and so does kit fed from a water butt or tank. Second, the ban is written for householders, businesses using water for their day-to-day trade are exempt. Thames Water’s own legal notice spells this out: a hosepipe may still be used to clean “walls and windows of domestic premises, paths or patios; or other outdoor artificial surfaces, where this is done as a service to customers in the course of a business”[8]. South East Water’s notice carries a similar business-use position covering services like window cleaning and vehicle cleaning[3]. The full rules, the fines, and the professional exemption are all unpacked in our pressure washing during a hosepipe ban guide.

Can I use a pressure washer during a hosepipe ban?

Not with a mains-fed machine on your own property, a pressure washer plugged into your outside tap counts as a hosepipe under the ban. Professional pressure washing is generally exempt as business use, and equipment fed from a water butt or tank is exempt too. The full rules, water company by water company, are in our pressure washing during a hosepipe ban guide.
