Patio Material Cleaning Guide: Sandstone, Porcelain, Concrete & Natural Stone

How to clean every common UK patio material - wear signs, cleaning frequency, and when to call a pro per surface type.

Last updated: May 2026

Get My Free Quote Call: 01737 652 515

The quick answer: The right way to clean a patio depends almost entirely on the material it's made from. Indian sandstone and limestone need low pressure and pH-neutral cleaners. Porcelain handles almost any technique. Concrete is the most forgiving. York stone and natural flagstone need careful handling to avoid surface damage. Use the wrong method on the wrong material and you can permanently scar a patio that cost £80+ per square metre to lay.

This guide walks through every common UK patio material in turn - what it is, how to identify it, how often it needs cleaning, the warning signs of wear, and whether DIY or a professional is the right call. Key Facts: UK Patio Materials at a Glance
  • Most common in Surrey new-builds: Indian sandstone (60%+ of patios laid 2010-2025)
  • Lowest maintenance: porcelain (clean every 24-36 months)
  • Highest maintenance: limestone & Indian sandstone (annual cleaning recommended)
  • Most DIY-friendly: concrete slabs (tolerates 3000 PSI and most chemicals)
  • Most pro-only: limestone, York stone, natural slate (acidic cleaners and high pressure cause permanent damage)
  • Typical professional cost: £3-£7 per m² depending on material and condition

How to Identify Your Patio Material

Before choosing a cleaning method, work out what you're actually cleaning. Many homeowners inherit patios with no documentation. Quick visual checks:

  • Indian sandstone - Riven (rough, layered) surface, mixed colours within a single slab (rust, grey, gold, beige), feels slightly gritty. Slabs typically 600x600mm or random sizes laid in patterns.
  • Porcelain - Smooth or lightly textured, very uniform colour, ultra-thin grout lines (often 3-5mm), cool to touch. Edges are razor-sharp from factory cutting.
  • Concrete slabs - Uniform pale grey or buff colour, smooth or patterned (often imitating stone), regular slab sizes, sometimes shows hairline cracks or efflorescence (white powder).
  • Limestone - Smooth or honed surface, very pale (cream, biscuit, light grey), shows fossil patterns sometimes, slightly chalky feel.
  • York stone - Buff to brown, irregular thickness, often reclaimed from old buildings, riven (rough) top surface, dense and heavy.
  • Slate - Dark grey, blue-black or rusty plum, smooth riven layers, very dense, can feel cold even in summer.
  • Block paving (used as patio) - Small rectangular blocks (200x100mm typical) with sand-filled joints. Visible joint lines and a brushed sand surface.

If you genuinely can't tell, take a clear photo and request a free site survey - we can identify it during the free quote visit.

Indian Sandstone Patios

Indian sandstone is the dominant patio material across Surrey - it's been the standard choice for new-builds and renovations for the past 15 years thanks to its low cost (£25-£40 per m² supplied) and natural appearance. It's also the material most often damaged by aggressive DIY cleaning.

Why Indian Sandstone Needs Special Care

Sandstone is highly porous - water and dirt soak into the stone rather than sitting on top. That's what creates the dark, dirty look on neglected patios: it's not just surface staining, it's contaminants embedded 1-2mm into the stone. High-pressure jets can pit the surface, strip the natural pigment that gives sandstone its colour, and force water deep where it freezes in winter and causes flaking (called spalling).

Cleaning Frequency

  • Sunny, sheltered sandstone: Every 18-24 months
  • Average UK conditions: Every 12-18 months
  • Shaded or under trees: Every 12 months minimum

Signs of Wear on Sandstone

  • Dark patches that don't lighten - Embedded biological staining, often where water pools
  • Pinkish or whitish residue - Iron leaching or efflorescence from below the slab
  • Surface flaking (spalling) - Frost damage from trapped moisture, often along edges
  • Lifted slabs - Failed mortar bed below, not a cleaning issue (call a paver)
  • Loss of colour - Either UV bleaching or aggressive past cleaning

DIY or Professional?

Mostly professional. A consumer pressure washer at 1800 PSI can clean Indian sandstone if used carefully (fan tip, 30cm+ distance, sweeping motion), but the risk of pitting is real. Professional rotary surface cleaners distribute pressure evenly and produce a streak-free finish that's almost impossible to match by hand. Porcelain Patio Tiles

Porcelain has overtaken Indian sandstone as the premium choice in Surrey new-builds since around 2020. It's expensive to lay (£60-£100+ per m² supplied) but virtually maintenance-free thereafter.

Why Porcelain Is Different

Porcelain is fired at over 1200°C, which makes it non-porous - it has effectively zero water absorption. Stains sit on the surface and wipe off. It doesn't grow algae from within (it can grow on the surface but rinses off easily). It doesn't fade. It doesn't pit. It tolerates almost any pressure setting.

Cleaning Frequency

  • Sunny porcelain: Every 24-36 months (often just a hose-down annually)
  • Average UK conditions: Every 24 months
  • Heavily shaded: Every 18-24 months

Signs of Wear on Porcelain

  • Dirty grout lines - The main weakness. Cement grout absorbs dirt; epoxy grout doesn't.
  • Surface algae or lichen - Sits on top, washes off, doesn't permanently stain
  • Chipped corners - Impact damage from heavy items - not a cleaning issue
  • Cloudy haze - Hard water or detergent residue, not damage. Wipes off with mild detergent

DIY or Professional?

DIY-friendly. A consumer pressure washer at any pressure setting is fine on the tiles themselves. The main reason homeowners book a professional is the grout lines - they need a stiff brush, the right cleaner, and patience. We can clean a typical 30m² porcelain patio (tiles plus grout) in 60-90 minutes versus a full DIY day.

Concrete Slab Patios

Concrete patios were standard in UK gardens from the 1960s through the 1990s, and pattern-imprinted concrete (decorative concrete that mimics stone or brick) is still common in newer builds. It's the most forgiving material to clean.

Why Concrete Is Forgiving

Concrete is dense, hard, and chemically inert. It tolerates pressure washing up to 3000 PSI without damage, handles bleach, patio cleaners, and even mild acidic cleaners (though we don't recommend acid). Pattern-imprinted concrete is slightly more delicate because the surface coating can be eroded by aggressive cleaning, but base concrete slabs are nearly indestructible.

Cleaning Frequency

  • Plain concrete slabs: Every 18-24 months
  • Pattern-imprinted concrete: Every 12-18 months
  • Shaded concrete: Every 12 months (algae grows fast on the rough surface)

Signs of Wear on Concrete

  • Efflorescence (white powder) - Cosmetic only, washes off with pressure cleaning
  • Hairline cracks - Normal in older concrete, not a cleaning concern
  • Faded colour (pattern-imprinted) - The colour-hardener layer is wearing through, indicating the patio needs resealing not just cleaning
  • Green/black algae stains - Standard biological growth, cleans off easily
  • Pitting or scaling - Frost damage on poorly-mixed older concrete; cleaning won't fix it

DIY or Professional?

DIY is realistic. Plain concrete is the most beginner-friendly patio material. A 1800-2200 PSI consumer pressure washer with a surface cleaner attachment will produce decent results. The main reasons to use a professional are time (we're 4x faster), pattern-imprinted concrete (which needs lower pressure and a specialist seal), and oil or rust stains that require pre-treatment.

Limestone Patios

Limestone is a sedimentary stone that became popular in mid-2000s landscape design - typically pale cream, biscuit, or light grey, often with visible fossil patterns. It looks superb when new but is the most chemically sensitive patio material in common UK use.

Why Limestone Needs Specialist Care

Limestone is composed of calcium carbonate, which reacts with acid. Acid rain (UK rainfall is mildly acidic), acidic patio cleaners, and even some patio cleaners that are safe on sandstone will etch limestone, leaving permanent rough patches and dull spots. The only safe approach is pH-neutral cleaners and low-pressure water.

Cleaning Frequency

  • Sunny limestone: Every 12-18 months
  • Average UK conditions: Every 12 months
  • Shaded limestone: Every 9-12 months (algae thrives on the alkaline surface)

Signs of Wear on Limestone

  • Dull patches - Acid etching from rain or wrong cleaner. Often permanent without resurfacing
  • Pitted or rough surface - Long-term acid weathering, irreversible
  • Heavy algae and lichen - Limestone's alkaline pH encourages biological growth
  • Yellow staining - Iron content reacting with water; sometimes lifts with the right cleaner
  • Black spots - Lichen, very stubborn, often needs professional biocide treatment

DIY or Professional?

Professional strongly recommended. The chemistry is unforgiving and the wrong product (or too much pressure) leaves permanent marks. Most homeowners who damage their patio with DIY cleaning have a limestone patio. If you want to DIY, use only pH-neutral patio cleaners and a fan-tip nozzle at 1500 PSI maximum.

York Stone & Reclaimed Flagstone

York stone is a hard sandstone quarried mainly in Yorkshire and used for centuries in UK paving. Genuine reclaimed York stone is expensive and prized; modern "York stone effect" concrete is much cheaper but cleans like concrete, not stone.

Why York Stone Is Different from Indian Sandstone

True York stone is denser and harder than Indian sandstone, but it's still natural stone - it's porous, holds biological growth, and can be damaged by high pressure. Reclaimed York stone often has uneven thicknesses and irregular surfaces that pool water and grow lichen.

Cleaning Frequency

  • Modern York stone: Every 18-24 months
  • Reclaimed York stone: Every 12-18 months
  • Heavily shaded or near trees: Every 12 months

Signs of Wear on York Stone

  • Black lichen spots - Almost universal on reclaimed stone; needs biocide
  • Green or grey algae film - Standard biological growth
  • Lifting or rocking slabs - Mortar bed failure, not a cleaning issue
  • Worn-smooth high points - Foot traffic over decades; characteristic of reclaimed stone
  • Persistent dark staining - Embedded organic matter, often needs hot-water cleaning

DIY or Professional?

Mostly professional, especially for reclaimed. The lichen on reclaimed York stone is notoriously hard to shift without commercial biocides and hot water. Modern York stone is closer to Indian sandstone in cleaning approach.

Slate Patios

Slate is uncommon as a UK patio material but it appears in contemporary garden designs and in older properties. It's dense, dark, and dramatic-looking - and brittle in cold weather.

Slate Cleaning Notes

  • Cleaning frequency: Every 18-24 months (it doesn't grow algae easily)
  • Best approach: Medium pressure (2000 PSI), pH-neutral cleaner, never below 8°C ambient temperature
  • Wear signs: Surface flaking (always replace, never repair), white salt deposits (clean with mild detergent), faded colour (sealing helps)
  • DIY or pro: Slate is the patio material most likely to be damaged by DIY cleaning. The brittleness in cold conditions catches people out. Professional only is the safest call.

Block Paving Patios

Block paving is more common as a driveway material but plenty of UK gardens use it for patios, especially smaller courtyards. It's effectively concrete (modern blocks) or clay (traditional brick), with sand-filled joints.

Block Paving Cleaning Notes

  • Cleaning frequency: Every 12-18 months (joints fill with weeds and moss faster than slabs)
  • Best approach: 2500-3000 PSI on the block faces, then re-sand joints with kiln-dried sand once dry
  • Wear signs: Sunken or rocking blocks (failed sub-base), missing joint sand (needs re-sanding), weed growth in joints (treat with our weed removal block paving guide), efflorescence on new blocks
  • DIY or pro: Cleaning is doable; re-sanding properly is harder. A failed re-sand wastes the whole clean within weeks.

When to Get a Professional vs Do It Yourself

The decision usually comes down to material more than budget. Quick rules:

  • DIY is fine if: Concrete slabs, porcelain, modern block paving, and a clean patio that just needs maintenance washing. Hire or buy a 1800-2200 PSI pressure washer with a surface cleaner attachment.
  • Get a quote first if: Indian sandstone, modern York stone, or any patio over 30m². The cost of professional cleaning is often only 2-3x a DIY hire and you save a full day plus the risk of damage.
  • Professional only: Limestone, slate, reclaimed York stone, heavily soiled or lichen-covered patios, and any patio where you can't identify the material. The cost of damaging stone is greater than the cost of cleaning it properly.

Service Levels and What's Included

Whatever the material, a professional patio clean usually includes one or more of the following stages:

  • Pre-treatment - Biocide application to kill algae, moss and lichen so it lifts cleanly during washing
  • Mechanical cleaning - Rotary surface cleaner at the right pressure for the material (1500 PSI for soft stone, up to 3000 PSI for concrete and porcelain)
  • Joint cleaning and re-sanding - Essential for block paving, optional but worthwhile for slabbed patios with sand-filled joints
  • Sealing - Optional for sandstone, limestone and concrete; not needed for porcelain
  • Spot stain treatment - Oil, rust, BBQ grease and red wine stains all need different chemistry
