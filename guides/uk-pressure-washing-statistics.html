1. Market size & industry structure

The most authoritative top-level figure comes from the British Cleaning Council's 2025 Research Report: the UK cleaning, hygiene and waste industry is now worth around £72 billion, a new record, up from £67.7 billion in 2022. The same report counts 78,915 cleaning-sector businesses in 2025 (up from 77,535 in 2024) and puts sector employment at 1.51 million people, roughly 5% of the entire UK workforce.

Structurally, this is an industry of very small firms: 83% of cleaning-sector businesses are micro-businesses with fewer than 10 staff (BCC, 2025). Counting the self-employed and unregistered operators, IBISWorld estimates around 480,000 UK cleaning-services businesses in total, having grown at a 5.2% compound annual rate between 2020 and 2025.

A note on the pressure-washing-specific figures: the widely quoted £450 million+ exterior cleaning market, its 5-7% annual growth, the 65/35 residential-commercial split and the 80%+ sole-trader share all trace back to The Upsurge's 2025 industry statistics page, which does not publish its underlying methodology. We therefore label them industry estimates rather than official statistics. No ONS category isolates pressure washing, which is exactly why this report exists.

The growth trajectory is steady rather than spectacular: the BCC's headline industry value has risen from £67.7 billion in 2022 to around £72 billion in 2025, roughly 6% over three years, while the registered business count grew 1.8% in the latest year alone (78,915 vs 77,535). Set against IBISWorld's 5.2% compound annual growth in business numbers since 2020, the picture is an industry adding operators faster than it adds revenue, which squeezes the least visible firms hardest and rewards the ones that publish clear prices and respond fast.

What the structure means in practice: pressure washing has no dominant national brand. It is a local trade of sole operators and small teams, typically serving a 15-30 mile radius, competing on response time, visible pricing and reviews. That fragmentation is consistent with the BCC's 83% micro-business figure and IBISWorld's near-half-million business count, and it is why the price dispersion documented in section 3 can persist: no national player exists to arbitrage a 2.2x gap between neighbouring towns.

2. Demand & seasonality

The cleanest single demand signal is marketplace search volume: consumers ran more than 28,000 searches for pressure washing on Checkatrade in 2025 (Checkatrade, 2025), and that is one platform among many.

Demand is strongly seasonal. Industry commentary suggests up to 70% of annual pressure washing jobs fall between March and September (The Upsurge, unsourced estimate, so treat as indicative). Demand peaks in spring and summer as homeowners tackle winter grime; we make no claims about seasonal price changes because no supportable data exists on that point.

3. Pressure washing prices by UK region: our original analysis

Because no official price statistics exist for pressure washing, we analysed 200 town-level prices and 12 published price lists across 6 UK regions to build the most complete regional picture available. The town-level data comes from the HaMuch driveway cleaning cost table (200 UK towns); the firm data comes from price lists that working pressure washing companies publish on their own websites, linked in full below.

What the data shows (Same Day JetWash analysis, July 2026)
  • National average driveway clean: £199 (median £194) across 200 towns
  • London & South East towns average £207 vs £183 in Northern England: southern firms roughly 13-17% dearer
  • Cheapest-to-dearest town spread is 2.2x: Poole £151 vs Taunton £332
  • Scotland is not cheap at job level: Scottish towns average £238; Northern Ireland is cheapest at ~£170
  • Across 9 firms publishing per-m² rates, standard driveway cleaning clusters at £2-£5/m² in every region
  • Minimum call-outs run £75 to £200, most commonly £100-£145 (7 of 12 firms)

Three findings stand out. First, town-level variation is bigger than region-level variation: the 2.2x spread between Poole (£151) and Taunton (£332) dwarfs the 13-17% gap between North and South, and both of those towns are in the same broad region. Where you live matters, but which firms serve your postcode matters more.

Second, Scotland defies its reputation as a cheap market. Bark's per-m² bands put Scotland and Northern Ireland lowest (£1.50-£2.50/m²), yet at whole-job level Scottish towns average £238, with Dundee (£312) and Edinburgh (£278) among the dearest in the UK and Glasgow (£211) closer to the national mean. Labour rates help explain it: HaMuch lists cleaners at £27/hour in Edinburgh against a £20 UK average (London £21, Manchester £18, Birmingham £17).

Third, the London premium is consistent across independent sources: +33% on hourly rates (MyJobQuote: London £70-£90/hour vs Liverpool, Edinburgh and Glasgow at £50-£65), roughly £45 more per driveway job than the North (Checkatrade, May 2026), and the top per-m² band on Bark (£2.50-£4.00/m² for London and the South East).

A quirk of the trade worth understanding before you compare quotes: on small jobs, the minimum call-out is usually the binding number, not the per-m² rate. At the firm-published £2-£5/m², a 25m² single drive works out at £50-£125 of metered work, below the £100-£145 minimum that 7 of our 12 firms charge. Small drives therefore pay the minimum in every region, which is a large part of why job-level averages (£199 across 200 towns) sit well above what naive per-m² arithmetic suggests, and why the higher cost-guide figures in section 4 are not simply wrong.

4. What firms actually charge vs what cost guides say

Here is the most useful finding in this report for anyone comparing quotes: the big cost guides quote up to five times what pressure washing firms actually publish. Checkatrade's driveway cleaning cost guide lists £8.50-£15.50 per m² with typical jobs at £250-£400, while across the 9 firms in our dataset that publish per-m² rates, standard driveway cleaning clusters at £2-£5 per m² in every region, and Bark's bands bottom out at £1.50/m².

The honest reading is that both are real numbers measuring different things. Cost-guide figures tend to bake in call-out minimums, small-job economics, add-ons like sealing, and post-job invoicing rather than headline rates. Published firm rates are the advertised price for straightforward work. Other guides sit between the two: MyJobQuote (May 2026) puts the average driveway power wash at £300, with size bands of £100-£160 for 30m², £150-£250 for 60m² and £225-£350 for 90m², while LandscapingCost (2025) quotes roughly £2-£4 per m², matching the firm-published cluster.

How to use these numbers when comparing quotes

The spread between guide figures and firm rates makes three checks worth doing on any quote. First, ask for the per-m² rate and the minimum charge separately: a "£3/m²" headline with a £200 minimum is a very different offer from £3/m² with a £75 minimum, and our dataset contains both (JetReady and Match Pressure Washing at the top of that minimum range, Glasgow Driveway Cleaning at the bottom).

Second, ask what is included, because the biggest hidden variable in driveway pricing is re-sanding of block paving joints. In our 12-firm dataset it appears three ways: bundled into a higher rate (JetReady's block paving with re-sand from £5/m² against £3/m² for a plain wash), charged as an add-on (Glasgow Driveway Cleaning at £1/m², R&T in Wales at £3/m²), or wrapped into a job price (JWF's £150-£250 for block paving including re-sand). Two quotes £60 apart may be identical once the sand is counted.

Third, treat sealing as a separate purchase. Published add-on rates run from £1.50/m² (Match Pressure Washing, Leeds) to £6/m² (Glasgow Driveway Cleaning), and MyJobQuote prices a 60m² clean-and-seal at £300-£450 against £150-£250 for the clean alone, so sealing roughly doubles the job.

5. Hosepipe bans & the 2026 weather backdrop

July 2026 handed the industry its biggest operational story in years: three hosepipe bans came into force within nine days, covering roughly 7 million water customers across Kent, Hampshire, the Isle of Wight and the East of England.

One widely shared claim needs correcting: reports that Yorkshire Water introduced a ban on 11 July 2026 appear to recycle its 2025 ban, which was lifted on 10 December 2025; the Lawn by Season restrictions tracker lists no live Yorkshire ban. Rumoured further restrictions elsewhere remained unconfirmed at the time of writing, so we do not list them.

What a ban means for pressure washing: a mains-fed pressure washer counts as a hosepipe for domestic users under the restrictions, but professional and commercial use is generally exempt (Drawpie, July 2026), one reason enquiries shift from DIY to the trade during bans. We cover the practicalities in our pressure washing during a hosepipe ban guide, plus the local picture in Surrey's hosepipe ban status.

The commercial effect cuts two ways. The banned regions overlap the UK's most expensive pressure washing market (London and the South East average £207 a job in our town-level analysis), and the domestic-versus-professional distinction pushes work towards the trade: a household that cannot legally run a mains-fed washer can still hire a firm that is exempt or works from tanked water. At the same time, the headline water numbers give professionals a story to tell, because a domestic hosepipe runs at roughly 1,000 litres per hour (ITV News, July 2026), so firms that meter their water use or carry their own supply can quantify the saving.

The weather backdrop explains the bans. The Met Office confirmed spring 2026 as the warmest on record for England and Wales (England mean 10.41°C), with southern England receiving just 50% of normal rainfall (its 8th driest spring) and Kent and Suffolk only a third of average. Kew Gardens hit 35.1°C on 26 May. That followed 2025 being the UK's warmest and sunniest year on record, a double record. Hot, dry, high-UV summers accelerate the algae, moss and biofilm cycles that drive cleaning demand, while simultaneously restricting how domestic users can respond.

The best available figure covers the capital: 44% of London homes have private off-street parking (English Housing Survey 2022, via the London Assembly, Nov 2024). 7. Business economics

Pressure washing remains one of the cheapest service businesses to start in the UK. A full startup breakdown by Pure Seal Services puts the all-in cost at £2,500-£4,000 for machine, water tank, surface cleaner, chemicals and insurance (Pure Seal Services, 2025).

Earnings figures should be treated as operator-reported ranges rather than audited data. Equipment supplier Equip2Clean quotes busy operators earning up to £500 a day in season, while Pure Seal Services estimates a solo full-time operator at £35,000-£60,000 a year after expenses. Both publishers sell to would-be operators, so read the ranges with that in mind.

Two structural facts anchor those claims. First, the trade's labour-cost floor: general cleaner rates on HaMuch run £17-£27 per hour by city (UK average £20), with skilled exterior work priced above that (HaMuch). Second, the shape of the market: with 83% of cleaning businesses employing fewer than 10 people (British Cleaning Council, 2025) and minimum call-outs of £75-£200 across our 12-firm dataset, the model is high-margin day work constrained mainly by weather and seasonality, with up to 70% of jobs estimated to fall between March and September.

Putting the pieces together as a sanity check: at the £199 national average job (our 200-town analysis), an operator completing two jobs a day grosses around £400, which is consistent with Equip2Clean's up-to-£500-a-day claim for busy season work. Sustaining that across a year is the hard part, since up to 70% of jobs are estimated to fall between March and September, so the £35,000-£60,000 annual net range implies exactly what experienced operators describe: full diaries in spring and summer, and thin ones in winter. The low £2,500-£4,000 entry cost also explains the churn at the bottom of the market; the equipment barrier is small, but the customer-acquisition barrier is not.

8. Safety statistics

The UK does not publish an NHS count of pressure-washer injuries specifically, and any page quoting one should be treated with suspicion. The closest robust figure is RoSPA's estimate of around 87,000 UK garden DIY and gardening injuries every year (RoSPA, via Asgard).

The hazard is real: consumer and semi-professional pressure washers run at roughly 1,500-4,000 PSI, up to 80 times the pressure of a garden hose (~50 PSI) (Yahoo Lifestyle, 2025). UK industry guidance from the Water Jetting Association covers machines up to 2,600 PSI / 180 bar in routine surface cleaning. At those pressures a concentrated jet can cut skin, strip pointing and etch soft stone, which is why technique matters as much as power; see our guide on whether pressure washing can damage a driveway.

Methodology & sources

This report was compiled on 12 July 2026 and is reviewed quarterly. It combines two kinds of evidence:

1. Published statistics from named public sources. Every third-party figure on this page is linked inline to the publisher. The principal sources and their publication dates are: British Cleaning Council Research Report (Mar 2025); IBISWorld UK Cleaning Services (Jun 2025); Checkatrade cost guides (2025-26); MyJobQuote cost guides (May 2026); The Upsurge industry statistics (Jul 2025, unaudited estimates flagged as such); Squared Money Home Improvement Index (2025); the 2025 UK Houzz & Home Report; Met Office climate summaries (Jan and Jun 2026); ONS garden access data (May 2020); the London Assembly housing stock report (Nov 2024); water company announcements from South East Water, Southern Water (via ITV News) and Anglian Water (Jul 2026); RoSPA garden accident figures (via Asgard); and the Water Jetting Association.

2. Our own analysis. The regional pricing section is original work by Same Day JetWash: we aggregated the HaMuch 200-town driveway cleaning cost table into regional and national averages, and separately compiled 12 published price lists from working pressure washing firms across 6 UK regions (all linked in section 3), comparing both against marketplace bands from Bark and Airtasker and city rates from MyJobQuote.

Data collection notes. Every source URL on this page was visited and verified on 12 July 2026, with one exception: Checkatrade blocks automated access to its cost guides, so its figures were captured from search-result snippets of the live pages and should be re-verified against the guides directly if precision matters. Firm price lists reflect what each company published in July 2026; firms change their rates without notice, so treat the 12-firm table as a dated snapshot rather than live pricing. Regional groupings follow each firm's own stated service area.

Caveats, stated plainly. No official statistics exist for UK pressure washing prices; everything here is guide, marketplace or firm-published data. Marketplace figures carry known biases: HaMuch quotes typically exclude VAT and call-out minimums and its collection methodology is not published; Airtasker averages skew low because task-platform jobs are small, so we never blend them with HaMuch figures. Where sources conflict (cost guides vs firm rates, the Yorkshire Water ban claim), we present both sides rather than picking a winner. Figures flagged as industry estimates have no published methodology behind them. Our own Surrey price list is excluded from the independent dataset.
