Why Horley surfaces get dirty, and how the estates change the job

Horley sits on flat, low-lying Weald Clay, the heavy, slow-draining substrate the British Geological Survey maps under the whole town[1]. Clay holds water where chalk lets it drain away, so the valley air stays damp and damp is exactly what moss and algae want. Drive up to the North Downs and drives dry out between showers; down here on the Weald the shaded half of a drive or the north-facing patio greens over first, every time. The Environment Agency also maps surface-water flood pockets through central Horley, the Burstow Stream corridor and out toward Smallfield[2], which is why we bund the rinse and dispose to foul drains on the lower-lying streets rather than flushing degreaser into a gully. What that means surface by surface:

  • Block paving on the new estates. Westvale Park off Bonehurst Road (1,510 homes going up under Crest Nicholson, Persimmon, Taylor Wimpey and A2Dominion) and The Acres at Langshott (710 Barratt and David Wilson homes) are almost entirely block paving. The joints are the weak point: sand washes out, weeds and moss move in, and the whole drive looks tired even where the bricks are sound. We wash, kill the weeds and re-sand with kiln dried sand as standard. More on our block paving cleaning page.
  • Concrete and tarmac on the older streets. Meath Green (once Moy Grene, some of the oldest homes in Horley, most rebuilt from the late 1950s) and the terraces near the station mostly have small concrete or tarmac forecourts. Quick jobs, usually at or near the £80 minimum, and the difference between grey-black and original colour is startling.
  • Patios out the back. Sandstone, porcelain and slate patios on the newer developments pick up black spot and slippery algae in the damp Weald air. We treat and wash them at a pressure the stone can take, then rinse the residue away.
  • Render and decking. Painted and silicone render on the town-centre semis wants a soft wash, not a jet, so we run low pressure and biocide. Timber and composite decking gets the algae stripped off before it turns into a winter slip hazard.
  • Commuter oil staining. Horley is a commuter town wrapped around a mainline station and Gatwick, so company-car oil drips are the single most common “not-just-moss” problem on RH6 drives. We pre-treat oil before the surface cleaner goes anywhere near it, so it lifts rather than grinding in.
