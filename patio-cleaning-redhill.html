Indian sandstone, limestone, concrete and block-paved patios in Redhill

Redhill sits on the floor of the Vale of Holmesdale, the low corridor between the North Downs Chalk and the Lower Greensand ridge, a boundary the British Geological Survey maps straight through the town[1]. Valley-floor air holds its damp, and damp is what algae, moss and black spot want. Drive up onto the chalk at Banstead and patios stay drier for longer; down here the shaded half of a patio greens over first, every time. What that means for each surface:

  • Indian sandstone and limestone in the older gardens. Plenty of Redhill gardens, especially around the town centre and the Redstone Hill conservation area, were landscaped in natural stone. It looks lovely and it also grows things. Full pressure scours the surface and strips the joints, so we work it low and slow with a surface cleaner and let the chemistry lift the green. The method is written up in our Indian sandstone cleaning guide.
  • Black spot on the shaded patios. Small black dots that don’t scrub off are black spot lichen, rooted into the stone rather than sitting on it. We find it constantly on shaded RH1 sandstone. A pressure washer alone will not shift it and blasting spreads the spores, so it needs a hypochlorite treatment with proper dwell time. More on it below and in our black spot guide.
  • Concrete slabs on the newer estates. Merstham, Salfords and the newer RH1 developments run to concrete or riven-effect slabs. These clean up fast, often at or near the £80 minimum, and the difference between grey-green and its original colour is startling.
  • Block-paved patios. Some RH1 patios are laid in the same blocks as the drive. The joints are the weak point: sand washes out, moss moves in. We wash, kill the weeds and re-sand the joints where needed, priced as an option so you only pay for what you ask for.

Black spot: the one to catch before summer

If your Redhill sandstone has small black dots that don’t shift when you scrub, that’s black spot lichen, and it’s the single most common patio problem we’re called out for in RH1. It’s rooted into the stone, not on it, so a pressure washer alone will not remove it, and blasting at it spreads the spores and roughs up the surface so it comes back thicker. The honest answer is chemistry: a sodium hypochlorite treatment with proper dwell time after the wash, quoted on top of the standard clean so you know the figure before we start.

Caught early it’s a straightforward add-on. Left for years it can get so established the stone never comes fully back, which is exactly why late spring and summer are the time to deal with it. The full story, what it is, what the DIY tubs cost and what we charge, is in our guide to black spot on Indian sandstone.

What’s included in a Redhill patio clean

  1. Walk the patio first. Surface, condition, pointing, black spot, drainage and any loose or cracked slabs. If a repair is what you actually need, we say so rather than wash over the problem.
  2. Pre-treat. Moss and algae get a sodium hypochlorite treatment before the water goes near it. The dilution and dwell time do the work, not brute pressure, which matters even more on soft natural stone.
  3. Wash at the right pressure. Surface cleaner across the field of the patio for an even, stripe-free finish, then the lance for edges, steps and the awkward bits by the borders. Lower pressure on sandstone and limestone.
  4. Treat and finish. A biocide over the cleaned surface kills the spores in the joints so it stays clean, and block-paved patios can be re-sanded where needed.
  5. Tidy up. Slurry and debris bagged and taken away, paths and borders rinsed down. We leave the patio clean and the rest of the garden no worse than we found it, no tide line of slurry across your lawn.

Optional extras, priced honestly: black spot treatment (quoted on top after we’ve seen how established it is) and sealing after the clean. Half of Surrey patios don’t need sealing at all, and sealing porous sandstone in the wrong season traps moisture, so we’ll only quote it where it earns its keep.
