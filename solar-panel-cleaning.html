Do solar panels actually need cleaning, doesn’t the rain do it?

The short answer: Yes, cleaning dirty solar panels increases output. Dirty UK panels typically lose around 2–7% of their output[5], and on a string-inverter system a single bird dropping can drag a panel down 20–30%. Rain doesn’t fix it, it just smears the grime into a thin cloudy film. An £80–£150 clean[1] usually pays for itself in recovered generation over the year.

Rain leaves the worst contaminants behind. Bird droppings, lichen, moss, pollen and traffic film all sit on the glass and block light, and the loss climbs to 10–15% in dusty or urban spots and up to ~25% in severe cases[5]. The droppings hurt most because on a string system the whole run is limited by its weakest cell, so one cell in shade or under muck drags the rest down with it.

How dirty your panels get depends on where they are

Honest truth: where your panels sit changes how dirty they get and how often they need doing. A bungalow array tucked under trees soils a lot faster than an open two-storey roof, and that changes the price and the cleaning rhythm. Why pure / deionised water, never detergents or pressure

The front glass on a PV panel carries a delicate anti-reflective coating. Abrasive pads, scouring and harsh chemicals can scratch it and reduce output, so we use only soft non-abrasive brushes and water. We run mains water through reverse-osmosis / deionising filtration to strip out the dissolved minerals, that’s what lets it dry spot-free and streak-free with no detergent, so there’s no residue left behind to attract more dirt. We never pressure-wash the panels: high pressure can force water past the seals and frames and damage cells. Many PV manufacturers specify periodic cleaning as a maintenance condition, so a documented pure-water clean helps keep your warranty cover intact.

Why we never walk on your roof

We clean from the ground using a telescopic water-fed pole that reaches typical 2-storey roofs. That keeps our technicians off the roof and away from the live DC wiring. Solar arrays are energised by daylight and can’t simply be switched off at the panel, so non-contact cleaning from ground level is genuinely safer, for you, your property and the panels. The Work at Height Regulations 2005 require that work at height is avoided where it is reasonably practicable to do so, and a ground-based pole does exactly that, no ladders against the gutter, no walking on slippery PV glass. It’s also why we clean early morning or on overcast days when the glass is cool, to avoid thermal-shocking a hot panel with cold water. Where an array genuinely can’t be reached from the ground, we’ll tell you and arrange the right trained-operative height access rather than risk it.

Will cleaning improve my output, is it worth it?

Yes. Removing soiling restores lost generation. On a clean open array that’s usually a few percent; where there are bird droppings or biological growth it can be far more, because of the string “hot-spot” effect, one dirty or shaded cell drags the whole string down to its weakest point. Soiling also raises panel temperature, and output falls roughly 0.2–0.5% per °C of temperature rise, compounding the loss beyond the light blocked. Put simply: an £80–£150 clean[1] that recovers several percent of your annual generation typically pays for itself within the year. Check your inverter or monitoring app, if generation has quietly dropped, soiling is usually the culprit.

How often should Surrey panels be cleaned?

Once or twice a year for most homes, ideally spring and autumn. Clean more often if you’re under trees, near a busy road or the Gatwick flightpath, on the coast, or your roof is low-pitched so dirt and moss sit on the glass instead of running off. Across Surrey the tree canopy of the Mole Valley and North Downs plus heavy spring pollen tends to push frequency up. The easiest way to stay on top of it is to book a recurring spring + autumn clean so generation stays high year-round, just ask when you call.

Common mistakes with solar panel cleaning

Cleaning solar glass is more about what you don’t do than what you do. These are the four that cost people money, output, or both.

  • Pressure-washing the panels. A pressure washer is the worst tool for PV glass. High pressure can force water past the seals and frames into the cell laminate, and most manufacturers explicitly forbid it. Soft brushes and pure water only, never a lance.
  • Using a squeegee, scourer or washing-up liquid. The front glass carries a delicate anti-reflective coating, and abrasive pads or grit dragged across it leave micro-scratches that permanently lower output. Detergents leave a residue that actually attracts more dirt, which is why we use deionised water with no chemicals.
  • Climbing on the roof to do it yourself. A pitched roof plus a live, daylight-energised DC array you can’t switch off is a genuine fall-and-shock risk. Ground-based water-fed-pole cleaning keeps you off the roof entirely, in line with the Work at Height Regulations 2005.
  • Cleaning in hot sun, or not cleaning at all. Cold water on hot glass can thermal-shock a panel, so we work early morning or on overcast days. And “the rain does it” is the most expensive myth going: dirty UK panels typically lose 2–7% of output, more under trees or near roads,[5] and rain only smears it.

Most-asked questions from Surrey customers.

Do solar panels actually need cleaning, doesn’t the rain do it?

No. Rain just spreads the existing grime into a thin cloudy film; it won’t shift bird droppings, lichen, pollen or traffic film. Dirty UK panels typically lose 2–7% of output, and more in dusty or urban spots.

How often should I have my panels cleaned?

Once or twice a year for most homes, ideally spring and autumn. More often if you’re under trees, near a busy road, on the coast, or your roof is low-pitched so dirt and moss sit on the glass.
